## 1. About

### 1.1 What is AXL?

**AXL (Assisted Language and Math Learning)** is a system-led, AI-powered, teacher-supported initiative that helps children read. AXL is being deployed by educational systems and communities across India, covering thousands of schools and community centers.

Today, many children — especially in the global south — are engaged in an invisible struggle to be at the level of competency that their grade demands. This is particularly true of basic skills such as reading in their primary language of instruction.

---

### 1.2 AXL as Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI)

AXL can be leveraged as DPI. Key properties:

- **Complete data sovereignty** — organizations retain full control of their data
- **Plug-and-play** — easy to adopt without heavy customization
- **Low resource use & operations** — designed for low-resource environments
- **Robust and scalable** — handles growth across systems
- **Flexible** — adaptable to different contexts
- **Configurable architecture** — modular design for diverse use cases

### 1.3 How AXL Works: The Concept of Safe Spaces

**The classroom challenge:**

Consider a Grade 6 classroom in rural or semi-urban India. Students react differently to lessons — some participate actively, others lose interest or find it hard to keep up. This dynamic reflects a broader challenge in education systems everywhere.

Students typically fall into three groups:
- **Strivers** — manage to follow the curriculum
- **Strugglers** — try hard but face difficulties
- **Drowners** — at risk of falling behind and dropping out

Many children are first-generation learners without much educational support at home, which amplifies these challenges. Existing educational frameworks usually lack the necessary remedial measures to address each student's unique needs, leaving some children feeling left out and disheartened.

**The Safe Space concept:**

Children who feel left out need a non-judgmental space to learn and practice language skills. A personalized learning programme that provides caring attention and allows children to practice at their own pace creates such a "Safe Space."

The aim of AXL is to create a safe space where there is:
- No judgement
- No pressure
- No evaluation
- Abundant engagement, personalized guidance, and joyful learning

AXL leverages AI to create these safe spaces for language learning.

## 2. Offers

The AXL programme has one focus: to provide personalized learning assistance to children as they grapple with the challenge of reading, in their own mother tongues and in English.

AXL provides assistance through the following four offers:

### 2.1 Teacher Assistant at School

A teacher grapples with several challenges in a classroom. One key challenge is the varying levels of reading competence in a single classroom — having a single learning plan for children with multiple competence levels reduces the efficacy of teaching.

AXL provides a way for a teacher to:
- Assess the reading competency of her class
- Assign students to cohorts based on assessment results
- Get personalized recommendations
- Design lesson activities tailored to each cohort

This is achieved through AXL's discovery mechanism and can be accessed from a simple tablet.

### 2.2 School Practice Assistant

For the child at school, personalized help leveraging AI can create a safe space to practice reading skills. A computer — usually in the school computer lab, equipped with an internet connection and a microphone — can be used to practice reading through personalized guidance a few times a week.

AI helps focus on each child's unique reading struggles and mitigates them through practice.

### 2.3 For the Child at Home

Young children are constantly learning — the quest to master reading is not confined to the school environment. For children at home, AXL offers an **interactive chatbot** that can:
- Have conversations with the child
- Answer her questions
- Use generative AI to provide personalized assistance

The bot can be accessed at home using a parent's device.

### 2.4 Community Learning and Assessment Tool

These tools are also available for community users — individual schools and community organizations. Users can:
- Use the tools as diagnostic devices
- Design their own remedial interventions

For communities, AXL offers a **hosted version** of these tools, making it easy to adopt and administer.

#### 3.2.1 A Deep Dive into the Kalulu Reading Method

Teaching children to read requires a solid understanding of the science behind reading acquisition and a well-structured pedagogical approach. The **Kalulu reading method**, grounded in cognitive science and years of research, offers a comprehensive and engaging programme designed to empower every child to become a fluent reader.

Developed with insights from cognitive neuroscientist **Stanislas Dehaene** and adapted for classroom use by educational experts, Kalulu bridges scientific theory and practical pedagogy.

##### Understanding the Science Behind Reading

Before formal reading instruction begins, a child's brain is already organised for spoken language. Phonemes, vocabulary, grammar, and sentence comprehension are largely in place. The challenge of learning to read lies in activating this existing knowledge through a new visual pathway.

This process involves a specialised area in the left visual cortex — often referred to as the brain's **"letterbox"** — which becomes attuned to the shapes of letters. A circuit develops between this visual area and the areas responsible for processing the sounds of language (syllables and phonemes), allowing the child to map letters to sounds (graphemes to phonemes).

**Two main pathways for reading:**

1. **Phonological Pathway (Decoding):** Initially, children rely on decoding — sounding out each word letter by letter. This involves breaking down words into graphemes (letter combinations representing a single sound) and blending them to reconstruct the word.

2. **Lexical Pathway (Direct Access):** With practice, word recognition becomes automatic and words are processed as whole units. This pathway allows for rapid and effortless reading — all letters are processed simultaneously.

Both pathways are essential for fluent reading. While skilled readers primarily rely on the lexical pathway, they still employ decoding when encountering unfamiliar words.

Kalulu prioritises the development of the **phonological pathway**, especially in early stages, through explicit and systematic phonics instruction.

##### The Key Components of Kalulu

**1. Four Student Booklets**

Designed with a gradual progression in reading skills, building upon prior knowledge with every new lesson. Each booklet features activities like "circle the sound," "match the picture to the word," and "read aloud."

| Booklet | Focus |
|---|---|
| **Booklet 1** | Foundational grapheme-phoneme correspondences; single letters (e.g., "a", "b", "m"); short simple words (e.g., "am", "bat") |
| **Booklet 2** | Digraphs (e.g., "sh", "ch"); slightly longer decodable words and phrases (e.g., "ship", "chat with Sam") |
| **Booklet 3** | Trigraphs (e.g., "igh", "ear") and blends; more complex words and sentences |
| **Booklet 4** | Multisyllabic words (e.g., "sunshine", "fantastic"); passages that challenge fluency and comprehension |

**2. The Kalulu App**

Complements the booklets with digital interactivity and targeted practice:
- **Gamified Exercises:** Interactive games like "Tap the Sound" (tap correct letter-sound pair) and "Word Hunt" (decode hidden words)
- **Adaptive Learning:** Tracks each learner's progress, identifies areas of struggle, and adjusts difficulty. E.g., if a child repeatedly misreads "ch," the app prioritizes related exercises until mastery
- **Audio Support:** Each grapheme and word is accompanied by a clear audio pronunciation to reinforce phonological awareness

**3. "Bien Joué!" Games**

Three card and board games designed for play-based learning:

- **Bingo:** Teachers hand out Bingo cards filled with graphemes or decodable words. A grapheme or word is called out and students mark their cards. First to complete a row or column wins.
- **Petit Triomphe (Small Triumph):** A board game where learners progress by correctly reading graphemes, words, or sentences. Cards test decoding skills.
- **Pêle-Mêle (Jumble):** Players sort mixed grapheme tiles to form as many decodable words as possible within a time limit.

**4. Vocabulary Development Through Story Reading**

Story reading sessions are carefully structured to build vocabulary and deepen comprehension:
- *Pre-Story Preparation:* Select a story that aligns with recently taught graphemes and words
- *During Reading:* Pause to highlight key vocabulary with questions like "What does the word 'bright' mean here?"
- *Post-Reading:* Reinforce learning with activities like creating sentences using new words or drawing scenes from the story

**5. Show & Tell for Oral Expression**

Encourages children to share personal experiences, describe objects, and engage in meaningful conversations — fostering language development and self-expression.

##### Addressing Learning Challenges: Adaptability at its Core

Kalulu's inclusive design ensures accessibility for all learners:
- **Dyslexia-Friendly Booklets:** Larger fonts, increased spacing, and simplified layouts to reduce visual clutter and make decoding more manageable
- **Dyspraxia-Sensitive Materials:** Specially designed booklets with coloured digraphs and trigraphs to guide learners in segmenting and blending complex letter combinations
- **General Support Strategies:** Multisensory activities like letter tracing, tile manipulation, and colour-coded visuals to strengthen phonological processing

##### Beyond the Basics

- **Writing Practice:** Activities like copying, dictation, and sentence completion using decodable words and phrases reinforce grapheme-phoneme connections
- **Assessment and Monitoring:** Regular one-minute reading tests to track fluency and identify areas requiring additional support

#### 3.2.2 The Akshara Journey: How Children Learn to Read Kannada

Unlike alphabetic scripts such as English — which rely on letters mapping to individual phonemes — **Kannada uses an alphasyllabary**. The foundational written unit is the **akshara**: a symbol corresponding to a syllable.

##### The Structure of Kannada Script

Each akshara typically includes a consonant with an inherent vowel; other vowels are added using diacritical marks. Consonant clusters are often combined into compact, visually dense units.

Young learners must understand and decode a **much larger inventory of visual forms** and the rules governing how they combine — over 400 common consonant-vowel combinations, not counting rarer or highly fused forms.

##### Key Research Findings (Nag, 2007)

Researcher **Sonali Nag's 2007 study** (Journal of Research in Reading) followed Kannada-speaking students across early primary grades:

- The path to reading fluency in Kannada is **significantly more prolonged than in English**, even in favorable classroom conditions
- This extended timeline stems from the structure of the script itself — not from instructional quality
- Children typically pick up simple aksharas early but continue to grapple with complex forms into the **third or fourth grade**
- By contrast, many English-speaking children have mastered their alphabet and read simple texts independently by the end of first grade

##### Transparency vs. Extent

Kannada's script is **transparent** (consistent rules for mapping sounds to symbols) but also **extensive** (a large inventory of units to learn). Even a predictable script can present serious learning challenges when its size and structural complexity are high.

##### Phonological Awareness Development

In alphabetic systems, learning to read is closely tied to **phonemic awareness** — the ability to identify and manipulate individual sounds.

In Kannada:
- Children first tune into larger sound units: **syllables** (since aksharas represent syllables, this makes intuitive sense)
- **Phoneme-level awareness** emerges much more gradually
- Third and fourth graders in Kannada-medium schools demonstrated phonemic awareness levels similar to much younger English-speaking children

This is not a shortcoming — it's a reflection of the script's structure.

##### Pedagogical Implications

The interdependence between orthographic knowledge and phonemic awareness is one of Nag's most valuable insights. Educators should:
- Avoid treating visual/orthographic knowledge and phonological awareness as separate tracks
- Weave them together, gradually guiding students from syllable-based understanding toward more detailed phoneme awareness
- Align instructional methods with the learner's developmental path and the script's structure

The complexity of the Kannada script offers children an opportunity to develop deep, flexible literacy skills — with the right guidance, children unlock an entire system of meaning, sound, and form.

**Reference:** Nag, S. (2007). Early reading in Kannada: the pace of acquisition of orthographic knowledge and phonemic awareness. *Journal of Research in Reading, 30*(1), 7–22.

#### Numeracy Screener

The **Numeracy Screener** is designed to categorize students based on their learning levels, facilitating appropriate entry into the learning environment.

**Key features:**
- Covers content from **pre-KG to Class 5**
- Available in **English, Hindi, and Kannada**
- Questions developed using sound assessment principles, standardized for language adaptations
- Divided into **five levels** (1 through 5), corresponding to the curriculum for Classes 1–5
- Each level contains **12 questions** to ensure relevance and appropriateness
- Evaluators code answer responses using provided scoring rubrics
- Coded responses can be entered into Excel sheets for scoring and further analysis

*To get a copy, contact the team.*
